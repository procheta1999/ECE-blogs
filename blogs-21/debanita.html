CRISPR : Biopunk To Reality


Debanita Roy Bhowmick

A Gattaca-like future is not a dystopian myth anymore. It is closer than we think and I’m talking reverse ageing, living for centuries, a proper cure for numerous diseases like Alzheimer’s, superfood and designer babies. Sounds like science fiction right. The trendiest hot topic for the science community is CRISPR technology, mankind’s newest invention can modify/create any or every DNA sequence to alter a single gene or the embryo completely. Humanity has levelled up to the big leagues and we’re now is a step closer to ‘playing god’. The year is 2020 and the biotechnician’s favourite game ‘build-a-gene’ finally wins a Nobel prize.

What is CRISPR/-Cas9 ?

CRISPR technology is a simple and affordable way to manipulate and edit DNA, changing the face of genome editing forever. The uses of this technology are limitless. Researchers are studying new treatments for diseases such as cancer, blindness and Alzheimer's. Scientists are also studying CRISPR to develop more sustainable fuel and chemical production methods and improve food crops. CRISPR stands for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats. Our distant prokaryotic cousins, bacteria, are like vaccines that can protect themselves from bacteriophages, which are actually tiny viruses that attack bacteria. This process is called CRISPR/cas9, or more commonly CRISPR, which means regular palindrome iteration. When a phage attacks a bacterium, it injects its DNA-carrying phage into a human host cell like a virus. If the bacterium survives the infection, it will take a small part of the phage DNA and build it into itself The subsequent protection is similar to the way our bodies make and use antibodies. The acronym CRISPR above describes the process of incorporating phage DNA into the bacterial genome. Bacteriophages are the most populous organisms on earth!- DNA and transcribes it into a small piece of RNA, which is another genetic material similar to DNA. Together with a protein called Cas9 and several other factors, the RNA can find the DNA of the invading phage and cut it into two halves. Before the bacteria can destroy it again.

Little background information to spice things up

So, why are we so curious about the process of using tiny bacteria that we can't even see? How does this help us? The answer can be summed up in two words: gene editing. Obviously, bacteria have been using CRISPR, and scientists have known this process since the 1990s. It was not until 2012 that scientists Emmanuelle Charpentier and Jennifer Doudna described the biochemical characteristics of the CRISPR/cas9 system and described how the process can be used to manipulate genes in other organisms. In 2013, a scientist at MIT named Feng Zhang successfully used the CRISPR/Cas9 system to manipulate the human genome. How does it work?

The splendour of CRISPR lies in its simplicity. CRISPR is based on just two components: the molecular scissors, a CRISPR-associated (Cas) nuclease, and the GPS guiding it to the appropriate site, the guide RNA (gRNA). In nature, the gRNA includes two distinct segments of RNA: CRISPR RNA (crRNA) and transactivating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA). Synthetically, they can be engineered as one seamless fusion sequence known as single guide RNA (sgRNA). Once the target gene and Cas nuclease have been selected, the next essential step is to design the specific guide RNA sequence. Several software tools exist for designing an optimal guide with minimum off-target effects and maximum on-target efficiency. The following tools are the most popular guide RNA design tools available are Synthego Design Tool, Desktop Genetics and Benchling.

Application and future scope

Genome editing is of great interest in the prevention and treatment of human diseases. Currently, most research on genome editing is done to understand diseases using cells and animal models. Scientists are still working to determine whether this approach is safe and effective for use in people. It is being explored in research on a wide variety of diseases, including single-gene disorders such as cystic fibrosis, haemophilia, and sickle cell disease. It also holds promise for the treatment and prevention of more complex diseases, such as cancer, heart disease, blindness, mental illness, and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection. Endnote

Many people are concerned with the ethical, legal and moral aspects of this technology. Some believe that when this tech moves out of the labs to commercial use, it will be expensive which will raise a new term 'genetic inequality'. Experts believe that this could add to the pre-existing social dichotomy between the haves and the have-nots. But like all things currently existing in our world, this technology too has good and bad sides. A simple paracetamol tablet can cause nausea and in worst cases jaundice, but it also helps with our headaches and fevers. One can and maybe will use this technology in the future to create something thing deadly ( like a virus perhaps or gene weapons to aid bio-warfare). But this same tech has the potential to cure and remove cancer. History bears witness to the fact that every new invention was met with a radical backlash of the ignorant. Scientific truth never won over the ignorant by making them see the light. The stupid ones eventually died and the next generation grew up being familiar with it. No research or discovery can be entirely good or evil as George Bernard Shaw had rightfully claimed 'Science never solves a problem without creating ten more.'
