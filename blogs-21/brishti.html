E-Waste – The Rising Concern


Brishti Saha

Technology as we know it has drastically altered our lives in ways that we would have deemed impossible just thirty years ago. It has molded our very existence and has played an important role in human evolution. Considering the amount of time we spend with our smartphones and other gadgets in our everyday life, we would find it hard to function or work properly without them even for an hour. Imagine having to drive to a new destination without your GPS or your online song playlists to keep you company. The constant dependence of humanity on technology implies how difficult it is to live without it. Ironically this technical revolution which has taken over the world like a storm is dangerously tilting the global ecological balance.

Over the last few years, the global tech market of electrical, electronic and related equipment has grown rapidly, while the lifespan of the hardware products has become progressively shorter. Thus both the business sectors and the waste management sectors are looking at a new and problematic challenge of constant generation of e- Waste.

According to the statistics and the current trends, the amount of electronic goods will continue to increase exponentially over the coming years on a global scale. Microprocessors are a growing demand in this pandemic situation where the world is moving toward a vast network on the digital front.

In India, the number of digital communication devices that were sold exceeded 607 million in the past two years. In a nationwide survey, it was found that 119.2 million television sets have been bought or procured in the rural areas the last year. The total number of units of electronic and communication devices sold on the Indian market in 2020 were worth more than 15,652 billion Indian rupees.

Consequently, the volume of e-Waste grows at a rapid pace every year and is also said to be one of the most critical waste disposal issues of this century. To be more precise and accurate the United Nation has estimated that 20 to 50 tons of e-Waste is being generated per year on an international basis. Thus there is an immediate need to develop a proper way of disposal of such items.

Compared to the conventional waste, certain components of electronic products contain extremely toxic compounds, which can generate a threat to the biosphere as well as to our health. For example monitor screens normally contain hazardous and toxic materials such as cadmium, mercury, and lead while beryllium, nickel and zinc can often be found in different circuits.

When these parts are thrown in landfills or incinerated, they pose certain health risks due to the radioactive decomposition of metals they contain. The improper disposal of electronic goods which increase exponentially over the years can lead to the increase in possible damage of the human body resulting in elevated risks of cancer, tumours along with developmental and neurological disorders. W.H.O has claimed that constant exposure to radiation from irresponsible e-Waste landfills can cause joint pain and other disorders in adults and brain damage and anaemia in children.

In India, the e-Waste management shoulders greater impact not only due to the generation of our own waste but also constant dumping of e-waste especially old computer hardware from the developed countries.

At present Bangalore itself generates about 10,000 tonnes of computer waste on an annual basis and in the absence of correct removal, they sell them to scrap dealers.

The current demand is to aim at reducing the accumulation of used and discarded electronic devices and equipment. India as a developing country needs simple and a low cost technology that will help in maximum resource recovery in eco-friendly methods.
