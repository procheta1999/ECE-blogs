Taking a look back at the infamous pentium 4


Debanka Ghosh

This blog is a personal project of mine.I live and breathe in Computer Architecture, semiconductor technology, and all things related to it. The aim of this blog is to discuss about a mixture of topics related to modern day processors. My writing style will be a bit technical in nature some of the points will dive into specific topics that'll be more sophisticated for example:-Pipelining, superscalar OOOe, But I'll make sure to keep it short and simple for the broader audience and average layman.

In this particular blog I'm going to be discussing about one of my favorite microprocessor of all time the infamous pentium 4, 2nd only to power pc 620 . Infamous for the various reasons we'll be discussing in this blog.

The pentium 4 isn't a bleeding edge high end microprocessor by any means whatsoever, Infact this particular cpu is 2 decades old. Still I find the architecture and goals of this design extremely fascinating and how it changed the perception of clock speeds


THE MYTH: Higher clock speed(mhz) = always better.

The pentium 4 hits the market around November 2000 and it was the first major new x86 microarchitecture from intel since pentium pro's (p6) core. The p6 microarchitecture was dominant at that time and it came in its reincarnations the pentium Il, pentium lII. If anyone was paying attention during that , CLOCK SPEED SELLS. Intel definitely was paying attention and they kept mhz at the foremost of their minds. This was quite evident from intels promotional event, marketing to every last details of the processor architecture..


PIPELINING

Pipelining is extremely crucial in secreting every last drop of instruction level parallelism. Unfortunately due to the size of this blog its not feasible to discuss about Pipelining but in short it has to do with increasing the overall throughput of the particular machine. Alongside the increased throughput deep Pipelining is extremely helpful in making the processor reach stratospheric clock speeds. Although Pipelining comes with its own set of drawbacks and that is though increased latency and complexity. Because of these drawbacks, many critics of the pentium 4 microarchitecture, dubbed as "Netburst" by intel, have suggested that the staggering long 31 stages of pipeline was In Fact a gimmick-- a poor design choice made for the reasons of marketing and not performance. Intel knew that the general public associated higher clock speeds with higher performance, so they designed the cpu to run at enormous clock speeds that would prove detrimental to real world performance, power consumption and scalability. As it turns out pentium 4 critics were both right and wrong in spite of the predictions and assumptions that might sound absurd. The pentium 4's performance scaled fairly well with clock speeds, but they were right about the design philosophy and the origin of the pentium 4s deep pipelined design. In my understanding and it's pretty safe to say that's the actual case, even though intel will probably never publicly acknowledge that. The pentium 4s design team was instructed by the market ing team to design something that would reach stratospheric clock speeds which will Give intel a massive mhz lead over its rivals. When it comes to clock speeds higher is often better, but due to the industry wide problems of transitioning to the 90nm in general, proved to be problematic for the pentium 4 which relied on its higher clock speeds to achieve performance and maintain an edge over its rivals Another point to note here is that power Consumption doesn't linearly scale with clock speed. For example a 20% clock speed increase would require 50% extra power consumption thus the pentium 4 required a lot of additional power than the competition to maintain its trivial lead.


CONCLUSION

Regardless of the drawbacks, pentium 4 proved to be a successful product both in terms of performance and commercially, despite the public reception. The initial releases were problematic because of their inability to clock higher which stems from the inferior 130nm node. The further releases down the line were remarkable.This is due to the way pentiums advantage in certain types of applications specifically 3d and streaming media applications. Thus I feel like this particular architecture is fascinating in its own rights.
