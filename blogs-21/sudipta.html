HARVESTING LIGHTNING ENERGY : POSSIBLE OR NOT ?


Sudipta Ghosh

We don't need to 'create' energy - it is already there in lightning. What we need to find out is how to 'store' the available energy. A technology capable of cutting electrical energy needs to be able to quickly capture the high energy involved in a lightning strike. Several schemes have been proposed, but the ever-changing power ground-based rods attached to each power bolt are objective - too high, it will damage storage, be too low and may not be effective. Additionally, lightning is scattered, and therefore energy must be collected and stored; It is difficult to convert high-voltage electrical energy that can be stored into low-voltage energy.

  • 1. Each lightning strike has an average of only five billion joules, which is equivalent to about 1,400 kWh of energy if we assume zero losses in transfers and storage.
  • 2. Lightning strikes for more than a year are about 1,400,000,000 of which only about 25 % are ground strikes, per 5 percent of indoor clouds and cloud-clouds, and it cannot actually be used. It has only hit 350,000,000 lightning strikes that could possibly be used. Also, it is assumed that 100 percent of all lightning use, capture, transfer and no damage to storage, which is 490,000,000,000kWh / year.
  • 3. In 2009, the world used about 20,29,40,40,000,000 kilowatt hours - 40 times more than the electrical power that all hypothetically tillage-capable lands contain. So, basically, all the lightning that we can capture will provide the world with enough electricity for just nine days!

Apparently, lightning strikes the surface of the Earth all the time, and there are about a hundred lightning strikes every second. (estimates vary) But that is on a 'world' scale whereas thunderstorms occur in a given location only for about twenty days annually. If the available energy is ‘distributed’ all over the world, there is not much we can do with it. We can't store energy from lightning due to various reasons.


  1. It's highly unpredictable, we don't know at what exact time it's going to strike
  2. We don't know where exactly it's gonna strike
  3. The magnitudes of current and voltages involved here is too high for any of our physical devices such as capacitor banks or inductors to handle.
  4. There is no mechanism to store such a large amount of energy in such a short period of time as of current technology, like the fast charging or turbo charging cells these days.


Setting up of such systems viz. Lightning harnessing power plant is not economical as well. Even locally, the problem is we do not know when or where it will strike next. Collecting lightning charges and converting it to useful energy may be an almost impossible task. Assuming someone designs such a complex system of storage and distribution, it may not be cost effective. Equipment to capture electrical power in a strike only has to handle an extreme amount of charge in about 30 milliseconds. To handle this type of instantaneous power, heavy conductive rods need to be used with ultra-heavy-duty electrical circuits and storage super-capacitors Therfore, We're yet to harness lightning as a source of energy but in the future we might very well do that.
