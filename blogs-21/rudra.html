Ray Tracing V/ Rasterisation - And You!


Rudraneel Bhattacharya

So imagine this - you’re fresh in the tech market looking for the latest and greatest card you can buy. The kind of stuff that will let you 420 blaze it, as the kids say, in all the latest, glorious AAA titles.

Then you come across the inevitable world salad of GPU marketing buzzwords - RT cores, Shaders, Rasterisation performance, VRAM, GDDR5, GDDR6, Overclocking, etc. - you get the idea. For the uninitiated, this is a massive put-off; and the marketers don’t exactly have the best intentions as much as they have their inclinations set on making a quick buck off of an unsuspecting customer like you. But fret not - for this little blog here is just for people like you!

Before we get started, let’s do a little housekeeping. Back in the day, games were really not meant to be played like they are, they barely had any graphics and usually game systems like the old nintendos or the ataris were made using some rather old microprocessors with limited graphics and sounds - for that generation, however, it was the greatest thing ever.

Fast forward a few decades and now you have games that look and play like the real world - er, maybe minus all the absolute chaos that you can cause but that’s getting way too IRL for me IRL. Digressions aside, games and entertainment have become exponentially more taxing which has lead to the current market of GPUs with insane performances and inbuilt memories which a decade or so back seemed outrageous to say the least. Even the cheap, mass market consoles pack more punch these days than supercomputers of the old days.

In particular, modern GPUs [Graphical Processing Units] are essentially mini computers within your own computer which are responsible almost entirely for the calculations and processing that lets you see stuff on your monitor. It has its own cache, its own memory, and it’s own dedicated output. In layman terms, for the most part, the faster and more powerful a GPU, the quicker it can render out frames onto your computer screen, and this is what you call FPS, or Frames Per Second.

Now with some of the basics cleared out, let us slide right into the meat and potatoes of our little discussion as we start with good ‘ole Rasterisation.

What exactly is “Rasterisation”?

Rasterisation, unlike what you may think, is a fairly new method for rendering 3D scenes that essentially uses polygons to approximate real 3D shapes and lighting. This is quite apparent when you use things like anti-aliasing or anisotropic filtering - what you’re essentially doing is that you’re increasing the number of polygons or using software techniques to make it appear as if you were increasing the number of polygons. This results in a sharper and cleaner 3D render. In the case of video games or even 3D movies, this is much cheaper to accomplish and easier to implement with existing game engines and 3D visual tools such as Unreal engine or Blender.

Modern GPUs, even cheaper, budget oriented ones are really good at this particular method of rendering 3D frames. Chances are, if you’re playing just about any game and using any GPU that isn’t an Nvidia RTX (with RT enabled) or AMD’s Navi lineup (Radeon 6000 Series / Superresolution), you’re using some form of Rasterisation for all of what your monitor is displaying.

So with that done, what about Ray Tracing? What’s the big deal and why is it taking the market by such a storm?

Well, technically speaking, the actual computational technique for RT is quite simple really; Imagine how human beings “see” things - millions, if not billions of individual rays of light(photon streams) bounce off of real world objects and then our brain fills in the gaps by figuring out the details of the raw information it receives and viola! You get to see the world in all of it’s splendor.

Ray Tracing or RT, employs a very similar technique. We place light sources to make light rays bounce around objects, then we compute the exact way the object should look, how it’s shadows should be, just how much it shines - essentially a very similar process to how we see on a biological level, except it involves an obscene amount of computational power, some incredible algorithms and a lot of software magic and emulation to achieve the end result - which is, quite frankly, an incredible sight to behold. Seriously, if you’re any bit interested in this particular field you may have already perused through the absolute fountain of internet memes involving RTX ON / OFF, but let me assure you, corporate speak aside, the actual end results are stunning and are immediately obvious. RT is most definitely THE FUTURE of 3D rendering, it’s just a question of mass adoption and a function of time - in the meantime, you can already enjoy some gloriously ray traced animated movies that took something like 1000 days or so for each 3D frame to be rendered - which kind of brings it all back to why rendering games at a playable 60+ FPS with RT on requires such massive amounts of computational power and the obscene cost in the present market.

So where does all of this lead you, the consumer?

Well, if you really just want some decent card that can play games, don’t buy a card just for the RT aspects - you will be disappointed and will be left with a kidney (or possibly both) sold off.

In the long run, though, RT is definitely a huge game changer and something we should all be looking forward to, especially with the latest generation of consoles looking to have native support built in, which will, for sure, facilitate a lot of market adoption among developers and consumers alike.

Thus as we come to a close here, I hope that we all can leave a bit more geeked out than when we arrived here!
